Overdosing On Salt

SCH3U - Juan Torres + Keyon Jerome

Salt is a dangerous substance.

We all need it to survive, but most Canadians eat much more salt than they need and should have daily. But have you ever thought of the repercussions of eating twice the recommended amount of salt?

Characteristics of Salt

Salt is an ionic compound. Let us explain.

Table salt is produced in nature by a double-displacement reaction. An example of this would be HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → H2O(l) + NaCl(aq).

  • Molar Mass: 58.44 grams per mole
  • Stability: Very stable
  • Solubility: 36 grams per 100 grams of H₂O
  • Freezing/Melting point: 800.8°C
  • Boiling point: 1465°C

Where is it found?

With 33.7% salinity, the Dead Sea is one of the world's saltiest bodies of water.

In Canada, there are three major formations that are mined for their salt.

Those three formations are located in Western Canada below the prairies, here in Ontario, and in Eastern Canada.

Salt can be found almost anywhere in the world; in mines underground, on rocks, in formations... the list goes on. Because of its abundance and use in our bodies, salt has become a cheap, commercialized necessity.

Why do we need it? The chemistry and biology of salt

Sodium is an electrolyte, meaning that it forms a charged ion when dissolved in fluids - like blood.

When dissolved in a fluid, NaCl forms charged ions and anions. These charged ions and anions, called electrolytes, increase conductivity in the blood and body.

This conductivity help transmit nerve impulses, relax muscle fibers, and maintain proper fluid balance within the body. The human body can't live without some sodium.

How much you really need

For reference, each slice (⅛) of a large pepperoni pizza from Dominos Canada has about 170 mg of sodium.

According to the Government of Canada’s Food and Nutrition department, people between the ages of 9 to 50 should aim for a salt intake of 1500 mg per day. Children aged 9-13 should not go above an upper limit of 2200 mg per day, and adults 14-50 should not exceed 2300 mg (1 teaspoon) per day.

Did you know that McDonald's Chicken McNuggets in Canada contain more than twice the amount of salt as they do in Great Britain? This is the case for many Canadian foods and drinks.

How overdosing affects humans

Too much sodium can cause dangerous, even fatal effects.

When there's too much sodium in the bloodstream, water rushes out of the body's cells to dilute it. That's damaging to most cells, and devastating to brain cells.

As the cells shrink, they're torn away from their usual locations. Torn blood vessels and fluid build-up in the brain cause seizures and coma. Fluid can build up in the lungs, causing trouble breathing. Other symptoms include intense thirst, nausea, vomiting, and weakness. Kidney damage also occurs. (source: poison.org)

The salt of the Dead Sea contributes to drowning deaths because even a few swallows of it destroys the electrolyte balance in the body.

In the less extreme case of salt overconsumption, which most Canadians are victim to, problems can arise as well.

In most people, the kidneys can have serious trouble keeping up with excess sodium electrolytes in the bloodstream. As sodium accumulates, the body holds on to its water to dilute it. This increases both the amount of fluid in surrounding cells, and the volume of blood in the bloodstream.

Increased volume of blood (more water) in the bloodstream means the heart has to work even harder to continue pumping, and this puts pressure on the blood vessels; stiffening them. This then leads to high blood pressure, heart attack, and stroke. (source: Harvard University)

Additional information

Feeling NaCl-y? Haha, get it... salty...

  • A review of fatalities from ingesting salt (27 reports, 35 fatalities documented) reveals that the lethal dose of salt in children is 4-5 teaspoons (10 g of sodium), and 3-4 tablespoons in adults (25 g of sodium).
  • According to the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation in Seattle, dietary risks are estimated to be the leading risk for death and disability globally. Among those risks, high intake of salt is at the top. High intake of dietary sodium was linked to over 4 million deaths and 83 million disability-adjusted life years in 2015. (source: see above item)
  • Hypernatremia is a high sodium to water ratio in your body. It’s the medical term for salt poisoning. Hypernatremia typically causes feelings of thirst and lethargy. The most serious symptoms of hypernatremia result from brain dysfunction. Severe hypernatremia can lead to confusion, muscle twitching, seizures, coma, and death. Hypernatremia is often diagnosed through blood and urine tests.
  • The human body contains many salts, of which sodium chloride (AKA common table salt) is the major one, making up around 0.4 per cent of the body’s weight at a concentration pretty well equivalent to that in seawater. So a 50kg person would contain around 200g of sodium chloride – around 40 teaspoons. Since we lose salt whenever we sweat, it has to be continually replaced.
